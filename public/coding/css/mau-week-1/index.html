Book Review: The Way of Kings by Brandon Sanderson

This was a fantastic book to read. The author creates a complicated world with well-developed characters, weaves the plot back and forth between character groups (even various time periods with the same people), and never loses sight of the broader storey. This is a regular occurrence in fantasy literature, and I frequently lose interest in one set of characters while waiting for the plot to return to the group that has my attention. This is not the case with this book. Sanderson keeps each plot original and fresh. It's tough to be innovative in a genre when there have been so many masters, yet this author has succeeded.He does an excellent job of transitioning between different parts of the storey. When authors shift from one time period to the next (or one set of characters to the next) in the development of sub plots, for example, I sometimes forget specifics or have to go back and examine previous storey arcs. Sanderson knits numerous storylines together flawlessly, so this isn't essential. Even storyline aspects that appear impromptu or unconnected become crucial to the broader storey as the work proceeds. As the novel nears its conclusion, it appears to be picking up speed.I can't help but believe that the author's completion of the Wheel of Time series provided him with valuable knowledge into structuring and writing an extensive and well-constructed fantasy universe like this. He was already gifted, and he was given the opportunity to look under the hood of a master during a crucial moment of his authorship. Overall, I give this book a 4.9/5 as it has quickly risen to one of my favourite fantasy books.

Book Review: Words of Radiance by Brandon Sanderson

What qualities do we search for in a sequel? Personally, I'd like to see character and plot development. I'm hoping for more world-building and solutions to the problems that were addressed in the first book. Words of Radiance is the second instalment in the ambitious Stormlight Archives series, and it succeeds on all of these fronts, as I can confirm.Sanderson, once again, does an excellent job of weaving his magic system throughout the novel without creating info-dump passages. However, his world-building does not stop with the magic system; this time, we also learn about the Parshendi race and culture. I found the interludes in The Way of Kings to be jarring and often tedious, but — once again, I'm forced to eat my words! — the interludes in this book were far more significant. The inclusion of a point of view from Eshonai, a Parshendi warrior/general, provided us with much-anticipated information about their species. The Parshendi, you see, communicate through song and have various forms that can change depending on their needs. Throughout the novel, they begin to plan and adapting physically and mentally for war. The build up to this was downright thrilling and made for one explosive conclusion. I give this book a 5/5.
